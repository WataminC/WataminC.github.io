    Isomorphisms

    Definition

    Two group (G,⋅)(G, \cdot)(G,⋅) and (H,⊙)(H, \odot)(H,⊙) are isomorphic if there exists a one-to-ont(injective) and onto(surjective) map ϕ:G→H\phi : G \rightarrow Hϕ:G→H, such that the group operation is preserved; that is,

    ϕ(a⋅b)=ϕ(a)⊙ϕ(b) \phi(a \cdot b) = \phi(a) \odot \phi(b) ϕ(a⋅b)=ϕ(a)⊙ϕ(b)

    for all a and b in GGG. If GGG is isomorphic to HHH, we write G≅HG \cong HG≅H. The map ϕ\phiϕ is called an isomorphism.

    Theorem about Isomorphisms

    Proposition

    Let ϕ:G→H\phi : G \rightarrow Hϕ:G→H be an isomorphism of two groups, then the following statements are true.

    • ϕ−1:H→G\phi^{-1}: H \rightarrow Gϕ−1:H→G is an isomorphism
    • ∣G∣=∣H∣|G| = |H|∣G∣=∣H∣
    • If GGG is abelian, then HHH is abelian
    • If GGG is cyclic, then HHH is cyclic
    • If GGG has a subgroup of order n, then HHH has a subgroup of order n

    Theorem

    All cyclic groups of infinite order are isomorphic to Z\mathbb{Z}Z

    Proof:

    Suppose G\mathbb{G}G is a cyclic infinite order, and g∈Gg \in \mathbb{G}g∈G is a generator. Define ϕ:Z→G\phi: \mathbb{Z} \rightarrow \mathbb{G}ϕ:Z→G by ϕ(n)=gn\phi(n) = g^nϕ(n)=gn. Then

    ϕ(m+n)=gm+n=gmgn=ϕ(m)ϕ(n) \phi(m + n) = g^{m+n} = g^m g^n = \phi(m)\phi(n) ϕ(m+n)=gm+n=gmgn=ϕ(m)ϕ(n)

    Next we need to show ϕ\phiϕ is a bijective map. The process is similar to the way proving Fermat's Little Theorem.

    Homomorphisms

    Definition

    Two group (G,⋅)(\mathbb{G}, \cdot)(G,⋅) and (H,⊙)(\mathbb{H}, \odot)(H,⊙) are homomorphic if there exists a map ϕ:G→H\phi: \mathbb{G} \rightarrow \mathbb{H}ϕ:G→H such that the group operation is preserved; that is

    ϕ(a⋅b)=ϕ(a)⊙ϕ(b) \phi(a \cdot b) = \phi(a) \odot \phi(b) ϕ(a⋅b)=ϕ(a)⊙ϕ(b)

    for all a and b in G\mathbb{G}G. The map ϕ\phiϕ is called a homomorphism.
